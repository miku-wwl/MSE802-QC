What is a Complex Vector Space?

Before we get into quantum computing, we need to understand the "language" it's written in. That language is complex vector spaces.

Big Picture

Think of a vector like a list of numbers. In everyday life, a 2D vector could be [3, 5] — which might represent a direction on a map. In quantum computing, our vectors contain complex numbers and can have many more entries.

Definition — Complex Number

A complex number looks like a + bi, where a is the real part and b is the imaginary part. The symbol i means √(−1).

Examples: 3 + 2i, −5i, 4 + 0i (which is just 4)

A Complex Vector Space Cⁿ is simply a collection of vectors of a fixed length n, where every entry is a complex number. The superscript tells you the length.

Example — A vector in C⁴
V = [ 6 − 4i, 7 + 3i, 4.2 − 8.1i, −3i ]ᵀ
Student Tip

The little T at the end (Vᵀ) just means the vector is written vertically (as a column). It's the same values — just a notation convention. In quantum computing, column vectors represent the state of a quantum system.

Key Properties of Addition

Commutative: V + W = W + V (order doesn't matter)

Associative: (V + W) + X = V + (W + X) (grouping doesn't matter)

Zero vector: V + 0 = V (adding zeros changes nothing)

Inverse: V + (−V) = 0 (every vector has a "negative")

Operation 2 — Scalar Multiplication

Multiply every entry in a vector by the same complex number (called a scalar). Think of it as "stretching" or "rotating" the vector.

Matrices & Types

A matrix is a rectangular grid of numbers. In quantum computing, matrices represent the operations we apply to quantum states (vectors).

Analogy

If a vector is the current state of a quantum system, then a matrix is the action that transforms it from one state to another. Matrix multiplication is how that transformation is applied.

Important Matrix Types for Quantum Computing

Transpose (Aᵀ)
Aᵀ[j, k] = A[k, j]

Flip the matrix along its diagonal — rows become columns and columns become rows.

Used in computing inner products and norms.

Conjugate (Ā)
Ā[j, k] = conj(A[j, k])

Replace every complex number entry with its complex conjugate — flip the sign of the imaginary part.

Essential for working with quantum amplitudes.

Adjoint / Dagger (A†)
A† = (Ā)ᵀ = (Aᵀ)

Transpose AND conjugate together. Also written A-dagger. The most important operation in quantum mechanics.

Defines whether operators are Hermitian or Unitary.

Hermitian (A† = A)
A[j, k] = conj(A[k, j])

A matrix that equals its own adjoint. Diagonal entries must be real numbers.

Represents measurable physical quantities (observables) in quantum systems.

Unitary (U·U† = I)
U · U† = U† · U = I

A matrix whose inverse equals its adjoint. It "preserves lengths" — applying it doesn't stretch or shrink vectors.

Represents quantum gates — reversible transformations on qubits.

Identity (I)
I[j,j] = 1, rest = 0

Ones on the diagonal, zeros everywhere else. Multiplying any matrix by I leaves it unchanged — like multiplying a number by 1.

Represents "do nothing" in a quantum circuit.

Inner Products & Norms

To measure quantum states — and compare them — we need a way to "multiply" two vectors together to get a single number. That's the inner product.

Definition — Inner Product

For two vectors V₁ and V₂ in Cⁿ, the inner product is written ⟨V₁, V₂⟩ and computed as:

⟨V₁, V₂⟩ = V₁† · V₂

Take the adjoint of the first vector, then multiply (dot product style). The result is a single complex number.

Example in ℝ³ (simpler, real numbers)

V₁ = [5, 3, −7]ᵀ and V₂ = [6, 2, 0]ᵀ

⟨V₁, V₂⟩ = (5×6) + (3×2) + (−7×0) = 30 + 6 + 0 = 36

Definition — Norm (Length)

The norm (or length) of a vector V is written |V| and is:

|V| = √⟨V, V⟩

It's the "Pythagorean theorem" extended to any number of dimensions. In quantum computing, a valid quantum state always has norm = 1.

Example — Computing a Norm

V = [3, −6, 2]ᵀ

|V| = √(3² + (−6)² + 2²) = √(9 + 36 + 4) = √49 = 7

Why does this matter for quantum?

In quantum computing, the state of a qubit is a vector in C² with norm = 1. The inner product tells us the probability amplitude — squaring it gives the actual probability of measuring a particular outcome. This is the heart of quantum measurement.

Orthogonality — When vectors are "perpendicular"

Two vectors are orthogonal if their inner product equals zero. Think of it as two perpendicular arrows — they point in completely different directions.

Hermitian & Unitary Matrices

These two matrix types are the workhorses of quantum computing. Every measurement and every gate in a quantum computer relies on one of these.

Hermitian Matrices — Measuring quantum systems

A matrix H is Hermitian if H† = H (the adjoint equals itself). Key properties:

1. All eigenvalues are real. This is crucial because measurements must give real numbers (you can't measure "3 + 2i seconds" on a stopwatch).

2. Eigenvectors for different eigenvalues are orthogonal. Different measurement outcomes are always distinguishable.

3. The Spectral Theorem guarantees Hermitian matrices have a complete set of orthonormal eigenvectors — an "eigenbasis" for the whole space.

Unitary Matrices — Running quantum gates

A matrix U is Unitary if U · U† = I. Key properties:

1. Preserves inner products. ⟨UV, UV'⟩ = ⟨V, V'⟩. Applying U doesn't change how "close" two states are.

2. Preserves norms. |UV| = |V|. Valid quantum states stay valid after transformation.

3. Always reversible. U† "undoes" whatever U did. All quantum operations (except measurement) are reversible.

Real-world analogy

Hermitian = a weighing scale. It measures something and gives you a real number.

Unitary = rotating a globe. You can spin it to any orientation and back. The globe itself doesn't change shape — it just moves.

Tensor Products

This is the most important concept for quantum simulation. The tensor product is how we combine two quantum systems into one larger system.

Core Idea

If V describes one qubit and V' describes another qubit, then V ⊗ V' (V tensor V') describes both qubits together as a single system.

The dimension grows fast: combining a space of dimension m and a space of dimension n gives a space of dimension m × n.

Worked Example — Tensor Product of Two Vectors

Take V = [2, 3]ᵀ from C² and W = [4, 6, 3]ᵀ from C³.

Their tensor product V ⊗ W lives in C⁶ and is formed by multiplying every entry of V with the entire vector W:

[ 2, 3 ]ᵀ ⊗ [ 4, 6, 3 ]ᵀ = [ 2×4, 2×6, 2×3, 3×4, 3×6, 3×3 ]ᵀ = [ 8, 12, 6, 12, 18, 9 ]ᵀ ∈ C⁶
Separable vs. Entangled

A vector in the combined space V ⊗ V' is called separable if it can be written as the tensor of two smaller vectors. If it cannot be written that way, it is called entangled.

Entanglement is the famous "spooky" quantum phenomenon — where two qubits become correlated in a way that has no classical equivalent. It's the secret power behind quantum simulation and quantum cryptography.

Tensor Products of Matrices

We can also take the tensor product of matrices, not just vectors. If A acts on system V and B acts on system V', then A ⊗ B acts on the combined system V ⊗ V'. This is how multi-qubit quantum gates are built.

Chapter Summary — Key Takeaways
  • A complex vector space Cⁿ is a set of length-n vectors with complex entries. It's the mathematical home of quantum states.
  • Vector addition and scalar multiplication are the two fundamental operations. Together they define the vector space structure.
  • The adjoint (†) combines transposing and conjugating. It's used to define Hermitian and Unitary matrices.
  • Hermitian matrices (H† = H) represent measurements — they always have real eigenvalues.
  • Unitary matrices (U·U† = I) represent quantum gates — they preserve norms and are reversible.
  • The inner product lets us measure angles, lengths, and probabilities in the vector space.
  • The tensor product ⊗ combines two quantum systems into one — it's the mathematical foundation of entanglement and multi-qubit computing.
